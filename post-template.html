The Language We Never Finished Learning

June 2026 · 6 min read · 💬 12 comments
Photo for illustration — replace with your own or a licensed stock image per post

Some conversations end not because there was nothing left to say, but because saying it would have meant admitting it was already over. We circled the real thing for months — the way you circle a pothole you already know is there, swerving out of habit rather than thought.

I remember the last real conversation we had. Not the last conversation — that one was about logistics, about whose name was on what — but the last one that meant something. We were sitting in a parking lot outside a grocery store, engine running for the AC, and neither of us reached for the door handle for eleven minutes.

"We didn't run out of love. We ran out of the courage it takes to keep translating it."

People say love is a language, and I used to think that meant vocabulary — the right words at the right time, the grand gesture, the perfectly timed apology. But I think now it's closer to grammar. It's not about knowing the words. It's about knowing the structure well enough that you can improvise inside it without breaking the whole sentence.

We never finished learning each other's grammar. We got good at the vocabulary — I knew her coffee order, she knew which side of the bed made me anxious — but we never got fluent in the structure underneath. What she needed when she went quiet. What I needed when I got loud.

Somewhere, she is still fluent in someone else's silences. And I am here, still translating, mid-sentence, to no one in particular.
